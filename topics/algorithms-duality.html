Algorithms & Duality

Translate convex analysis into actionable solvers and interpret solutions through dual views.

Why Algorithms and Duality Matter

Convex optimization becomes truly useful once you can both compute solutions efficiently and certify their quality. Algorithms provide the computational pathway, while duality supplies certificates, bounds, and sensitivity analysis. Together they turn definitions into deployable tools.

Efficient solvers and dual certificates close the loop between modeling and guarantees.

First-Order Optimality Conditions

For a convex problem with inequality constraints and affine equalities, the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions are sufficient for global optimality and, under standard regularity assumptions (for example, Slater's condition), also necessary:

\[\begin{aligned} \nabla f_0(x^*) + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i \nabla f_i(x^*) + A^T \nu &= 0, \\ f_i(x^*) &\le 0, \; \lambda_i \ge 0, \; \lambda_i f_i(x^*) = 0, \\ A x^* &= b. \end{aligned}\]

Strong duality certifies that primal and dual optimal values match, so satisfying these equations means you have already found the global solution.

Dual Problem Construction

The Lagrangian collects constraints into scalar multipliers:

\[\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \nu) = f_0(x) + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i f_i(x) + \nu^T (Ax - b).\]

The dual function \(g(\lambda, \nu) = \inf_x \mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \nu)\) is always concave. Maximizing it over \(\lambda \ge 0\) gives the dual problem and a lower bound on the primal optimum. Matching primal and dual solutions provides certificates and sensitivity information.

As algorithms progress, the primal-dual gap shrinks to zero under strong duality.

Algorithm Playbook

Projected Gradient

Iterate along gradient steps and project back to the feasible set to enforce constraints.

repeat
    x_half = x_k - alpha_k * grad_f0(x_k)
    x_next = project_C(x_half)
until norm(x_next - x_k) <= epsilon

Subgradient + Dual Averaging

Works for non-differentiable objectives; dual averaging keeps a running mean of subgradients to stabilize progress.

Interior-Point (Barrier) Methods

Replace inequality constraints with a barrier term \(-\mu \sum_i \log(-f_i(x))\) and follow the central path by repeatedly solving Newton systems. Dual variables emerge from the linear systems, providing a primal-dual pair each iteration.

Worked Example: A Simple Quadratic Program

Let's turn the ideas into a compact 3-step flow.

Problem

\[\begin{aligned} \text{minimize} \quad &\frac{1}{2} \|x - d\|_2^2 \\ \text{subject to} \quad &\|x\|_\infty \le \rho, \end{aligned}\]

where \(d \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is data and \(\rho > 0\) is a bound. This is a projection problem: find the point in the \(\ell_\infty\) ball closest to \(d\).

Step 1. Stationarity

Convert the box constraint to component bounds \(-\rho \le x_i \le \rho\), then apply KKT.

\[x^* - d + \lambda = 0\]

Interpretation: \(\lambda = d - x^*\), so it points from \(x^*\) toward \(d\).

Step 2. Closed-form solution

The optimizer is just coordinate-wise clipping:

\[x_i^* = \operatorname{sign}(d_i)\min(|d_i|,\rho)\]

If \(|d_i| \le \rho\), keep it. If \(|d_i| > \rho\), clamp to \(\pm \rho\).

Step 3. Quick numeric check

Use the demo parameters \(d=(3,2.5)\), \(\rho=1.5\).

\[x^*=(1.5,1.5), \quad \lambda=d-x^*=(1.5,1.0)\]
\[f^*=\tfrac{1}{2}(1.5^2+1^2)=1.625\]

Binding coordinates have nonzero multipliers, which is the dual sensitivity signal.

Primal–Dual Monitoring Checklist

  • Track primal feasibility (constraint violation norms).
  • Track dual feasibility (KKT residuals).
  • Measure the duality gap to know when to stop early.
  • Log step sizes and barrier parameters for reproducibility.
