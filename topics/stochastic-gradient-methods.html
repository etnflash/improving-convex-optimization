Stochastic Gradient Methods

Scale convex optimization to massive datasets with unbiased noisy gradients.

Finite-Sum Objective

\[\min_x \; F(x)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f_i(x).\]

Full gradients cost \(O(N)\) per step. Stochastic methods sample one (or a mini-batch of) term(s) to reduce per-iteration cost.

SGD Update

Draw index \(i_k\) uniformly and use

\[x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta_k\nabla f_{i_k}(x_k).\]

The stochastic gradient is unbiased: \(\mathbb{E}[\nabla f_{i_k}(x_k)] = \nabla F(x_k)\). 

repeat
    sample i_k
    g_k = grad f_{i_k}(x_k)
    x_{k+1} = x_k - eta_k * g_k
until budget or convergence

Mini-batch Variant

With mini-batch \(B_k\),

\[g_k = \frac{1}{|B_k|}\sum_{i\in B_k} \nabla f_i(x_k).\]

Larger batches reduce variance but increase per-step work. In practice, hardware throughput and noise scale guide the choice.

Step-size Strategies

  • Diminishing: \(\eta_k = \eta_0 / \sqrt{k}\) or \(\eta_0/k\) for convergence guarantees.
  • Piecewise-constant schedules for practical training pipelines.
  • Polyak/averaging variants to stabilize noisy iterates.

Convergence Intuition

For convex Lipschitz objectives, averaged SGD often achieves \(O(1/\sqrt{k})\) suboptimality. Strong convexity with appropriate step sizes can improve rates in expectation.

Variance Reduction (SVRG/SAGA Idea)

Variance-reduced methods mix cheap stochastic updates with occasional reference gradients, recovering faster linear-like behavior on strongly convex finite sums.

When to Upgrade from Plain SGD

If data are finite-sum and repeatedly scanned, SVRG/SAGA-style approaches can converge much faster.
