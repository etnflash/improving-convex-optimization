Coordinate Descent

Optimize one coordinate at a time for scalable convex learning on sparse and high-dimensional data.

Core Idea

Instead of updating all variables simultaneously, coordinate descent updates a single coordinate (or block) while holding others fixed. Each step is cheap and often admits closed forms.

Problem Setup

\[\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n} F(x).\]

At iteration \(k\), choose coordinate \(j_k\) and solve

\[x_{j_k}^{k+1}=\arg\min_u F(x_1^k,\dots,x_{j_k-1}^k,u,x_{j_k+1}^k,\dots,x_n^k).\]

Selection Rules

  • Cyclic: iterate through coordinates in fixed order.
  • Randomized: sample coordinates for stronger expected-rate theory.
  • Greedy (Gauss-Southwell): pick largest predicted improvement.

Example: Lasso-Friendly Updates

For least-squares + L1 regularization, each coordinate update becomes a scalar soft-thresholding step, making coordinate descent extremely competitive in sparse regression.

Practical Strength

Works very well when features are sparse and coordinate-wise residual updates can be cached.

Block Coordinate Descent

Group variables into blocks and update one block at a time. This balances cheap updates and faster per-iteration progress compared to scalar coordinates. 

repeat
    choose block B_k
    x_{B_k}^{k+1} = argmin over block with others fixed
until convergence

Convergence Intuition

For convex smooth objectives, randomized coordinate descent often achieves rates depending on coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants. Strong convexity yields linear convergence in expectation.

Implementation Checklist

  • Precompute or cache terms needed for fast coordinate updates.
  • Compare cyclic vs randomized selection on your dataset.
  • Track objective decrease and coordinate gradient magnitudes.
  • Use block updates when coordinates are strongly coupled.
