Convex Optimization Problems

Combine convex objectives and convex feasible regions to guarantee tractable solutions.

Why Convex Problems Matter

Once you know what convex sets and convex functions look like, the next step is to pose optimization problems whose feasible region is convex and whose objective is convex. These two ingredients ensure that every local minimum is a global minimum and that efficient algorithms exist to find it.

A convex feasible polytope with a single optimal point. Any descent direction eventually runs into the boundary where the optimum lives.

Standard Problem Forms

Many convex problems can be written in the template

\[\begin{aligned} \text{minimize} &\quad f_0(x) \\ \text{subject to} &\quad f_i(x) \le 0, \quad i=1,\dots,m, \\ &\quad A x = b, \end{aligned}\]

where each inequality constraint function \(f_i\) is convex and the equality constraints describe an affine subspace. Special cases include quadratic programs, second-order cone programs, and linear programs.

Feasible Sets and Objective Contours

Visualizing convex problems often involves the feasible region plus level sets of the objective. The optimum occurs where the lowest contour touches the feasible set.

Optimal point \(x^*\) sits on the lowest objective contour that still intersects the feasible disk.

Example Problems

Least-Squares with L1 Regularization

\(\min_x \frac{1}{2}\|Ax-b\|_2^2 + \lambda \|x\|_1\). Convex because the squared norm and the \(\ell_1\) norm are convex; often solved with proximal gradient methods.

Portfolio Allocation

\(\min_x x^T \Sigma x - \mu^T x\) subject to \(\mathbf{1}^T x = 1,\; x \ge 0\). Quadratic objective + affine and inequality constraints make this a convex quadratic program.
