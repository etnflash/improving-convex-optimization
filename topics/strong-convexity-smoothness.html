Strong Convexity and Smoothness

Use curvature bounds and condition numbers to predict first-order convergence behavior.

Curvature Bounds

A differentiable function is \(\mu\)-strongly convex if

\[f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T(y-x) + \frac{\mu}{2}\|y-x\|_2^2.\]

It is \(L\)-smooth if

\[\|\nabla f(y)-\nabla f(x)\|_2 \le L\|y-x\|_2.\]

Together, these inequalities pin down how flat or sharp the objective can be.

Condition Number

The ratio

\[\kappa = \frac{L}{\mu}\]

controls optimization difficulty. Small \(\kappa\) means nearly spherical level sets; large \(\kappa\) means elongated valleys and slower gradient progress.

Quadratic Model Intuition

For \(f(x)=\tfrac{1}{2}x^TAx\) with \(A\succ 0\), \(\mu=\lambda_{\min}(A)\), \(L=\lambda_{\max}(A)\), and \(\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}\). This links geometry directly to eigenvalues.

Practical Reading

Preconditioning often tries to reduce effective \(\kappa\), transforming a narrow valley into a rounder landscape.

Rate Snapshot

For gradient descent with the tuned step size \(\alpha = 2/(L+\mu)\), one obtains

\[\|x_{k+1}-x^*\|_2 \le \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\|x_k-x^*\|_2.\]

The contraction factor gets worse as \(\kappa\) increases.
